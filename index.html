Introduction

Georgetown University's history is inextricably linked to slavery and the transatlantic slave trade. In 1838, the Jesuit priests who ran Georgetown University sold 272 enslaved individuals to plantations in Louisiana to pay off debts and secure the institution's financial future. Many of these individuals had ancestral connections to West Africa, particularly regions that are now part of modern-day Gambia.

This website aims to document and explore the historical connections between Georgetown University, the transatlantic slave trade, and Gambia. By examining these histories, we hope to contribute to ongoing efforts of truth, reconciliation, and healing.

Historical Timeline

1634

The first Jesuits arrive in Maryland, bringing enslaved people with them to establish plantations that would later help fund Georgetown College.

1789

Georgetown College (later Georgetown University) is founded. The institution's early financial stability relied heavily on the labor of enslaved people and profits from Jesuit-owned plantations in Maryland.

1838

Facing financial difficulties, Georgetown's Jesuit leaders sell 272 enslaved men, women, and children to plantations in Louisiana. The sale, worth approximately $3.3 million in today's currency, saved the institution from financial ruin.

2015-2016

Georgetown University begins to formally acknowledge its historical ties to slavery. The university renames two buildings previously named after the Jesuits involved in the 1838 sale.

2019

Georgetown students approve a referendum to establish a fund for descendants of the enslaved people sold in 1838. Research intensifies on tracing the origins of enslaved individuals to regions in West Africa, including Gambia.

2020-Present

Ongoing research, reconciliation efforts, and partnerships with institutions in Gambia to better understand the historical connections and support healing initiatives.

The Gambian Connection

Many of the enslaved individuals owned by the Jesuits and sold in 1838 had ancestral ties to regions in West Africa, particularly areas in modern-day Gambia. Gambia was a significant site in the transatlantic slave trade due to its position along the Gambia River, which provided easy access to the interior of the continent.

Key points about the Gambian connection include:

  • The Gambia River was a major artery for the slave trade, with European traders establishing outposts along its banks.
  • Many enslaved people were captured from ethnic groups like the Mandinka, Wolof, and Fula, who inhabited regions in and around present-day Gambia.
  • James Island (now Kunta Kinteh Island) in the Gambia River was a significant holding site for enslaved individuals before their transport across the Atlantic.
  • Ongoing genetic and genealogical research is helping descendants trace their ancestral connections to specific regions and communities in Gambia.
  • Cultural retentions and practices among descendants of the Georgetown 272 show connections to Gambian cultural traditions and practices.
